# Disclaimer

## What the grade means

The **Sovereignty Grade** assigned to each operating system on this site is an **opinion expressed against a public rubric** ([`RUBRIC.md`](RUBRIC.md)).

- It reflects how closely the operating system, as shipped, follows the principles of user sovereignty, neutrality, and general-purpose computing.
- It is **not** a legal certification.
- It is **not** an industry-accredited standard.
- It is **not** a security audit, a warranty of fitness, or legal advice.

The grade communicates, at a glance, how the audited software scores against an explicit, versioned set of criteria. Readers are free to disagree with the rubric, the weights, or the scoring — and to propose changes via pull request.

## How scores are reached

Every score on this site is produced by applying the rubric to publicly available evidence. Every score in `data/systems.json` is accompanied by at least one cited URL. No score relies on hidden information.

The rubric version used for each audit is recorded next to the audit, so that any grade can be reproduced or challenged by re-running the rubric against the same evidence.

## Challenging a score

If you believe a score is wrong, outdated, or based on misinterpreted evidence:

1. Open an issue in this repository with the slug of the affected entry.
2. Include the specific factor(s) you dispute.
3. Provide **counter-evidence** (primary sources preferred).
4. Propose a revised score with reasoning.

Accepted challenges are resolved by updating the entry in `data/systems.json` and recording the change in `CHANGELOG.md`. The full history is preserved in git.
